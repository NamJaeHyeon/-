Unprecedented Humid Heat Waves Unprecedented levels of humid heat are being recorded. It has gotten to the point that it is likely that some regions will become too hot for humans to inhabit in a few decades. The effects of this comnination of extreme humidity and heat have already kicked off several environmental disasters decades earlier than the previously estimated timeline. While the coronavirus pandemic has undoubtedly become the prime focus of the global community, increasing reports of natural disasters have made it clear that our environmental problem is still spriraling out of control. The year 2019 in particular has experienced abnormally high temperatures and humidity that together precipitated massive wildfires and death tolls. Before the enforcement of lockdowns and quarantines, populations in regions usch as Australia has been steadily shifting their internal clock to stay indoors during the day and operate during the night when it is cooler. It is the high humidity that has made conditions so unbearable. Normally, when it is hot, our bodies try to cool down by sweating. As the sweat evaporates, it disperses the body’s heat to the air. But the rate at which water or sweat dissipates into the air is determined by how much water is already present in the air. Sweat evaporates faster on dry days than humid ones, which is why we feel like it is hotter on muggy days, where the air is saturated with water. When the humidity of the air rises to a certain high level, our inbuilt cooling system becomes ineffectual and our bodies heat up. And when the body temperature reaches an extreme level, even organs can shut down as the body starts cooking itself. This is an exceedingly serious problem for populations living in the develoging world, where air conditioning is not widespread or easily accessible for the average household. The futre looks bleak, as the regions predicted to be hit most severely by the humid heat waves over the next few decades consist mostly of poorer countries where people would have less resources to fight the escalating humid heat. Vulnerable areas to the humid heat waves include parts of sub-Saharan Africa, South America, India, Southeast Asia, the Arabian Peninsula, and Australia. According to a recent study, if our planet’s temperature continues to rise at current levels over the next 50 years, approximately 3 billion people could end up living in areas too hot for humans. Currentlu, extremely hot areas like the Sahara region cover 0.8 percent of the Earth’s landmass. These areas are predicted to spread to 19 percent by 2070, resulting in a massive displacement of people that would be disastrous both economically and socially.
